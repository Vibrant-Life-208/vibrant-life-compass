# The Launch Pad Story

**A three-year formation for learners ages 15-18**
**Status:** v0.1 working draft - locked structure, Season One built deep
**Date:** 2026-06-24
**Origin:** Evoke Passion fleet meeting (8 voices) facilitated for Europa, toward a presentation for Jenna

---

## The spine (the one-breath version)

Launch Pad is three expeditions. Every learner walks all three before they launch - in whatever order their cohort is on.

- **Origin** *(the set-up)* - *Who am I?* (Know your story.)
- **The Forge** *(the problem)* - *What is worth solving?* (Find your work.)
- **The Helm** *(the resolution)* - *What do I do when there is no easy answer?* (Make the hard call - and love the life around it.)

Each year is a complete hero's journey. Each carries one constant thread: a learner-owned **Captain's Log** that deepens every year. We are not running a high school. We are forming people who can think for themselves, make hard decisions, and love their lives.

> *"We are not training grim problem-solvers. We are forming captains who tend the vineyard, play the flute, love their crew - and can still make the hard call."*

---

## How it works

### The program is a cycle. The learner is a line.

The **cycle** turns Set-Up to Problem to Resolution forever, like seasons. The **learner** walks their own Captain's Log from the day they arrive to the day they launch - that is their three-act story, and it holds no matter which season they entered.

Names are by lens, never by number. There is no "Year 1 / Year 2 / Year 3," because that would rank a learner who enters mid-cycle as "behind." A learner entering mid-cycle is **joining the story in progress**, never arriving late. You ride the cycle once fully around before you launch; you may join at any season.

The three seasons are named **Origin**, **The Forge**, and **The Helm** - an arc of agency (given, then forged, then at the helm). The story-beats (set-up, problem, resolution) are kept as a quiet descriptor so the structure stays legible to parents and learners.

### The weekly rhythm (all three seasons)

| Mon | Tue | Wed | Thu | Cadence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Launch** (guide) | Project work | **Launch** (guide) | Project work | **Fallow / hearth day** |

- **Two Launch days** with the Launch Pad Guide. The Guide is a **life-coach, not a teacher**: Socratic, holds questions rather than answers, has permission to say "I don't know - let's walk into it together."
- **Two project days** for self-directed work: core academics and high-school requirements, personal projects, and the season's writing.
- **A fallow / hearth day on a regular cadence.** Protected. Celebrated. Never earned, never filled. Rest, play, friendship, making-for-no-reason. This is practice, not reward.
- Every Launch day eases in with a self-directed project hour, then gathers for a body-first settle and a "how are you, really?" circle before the quest work, and closes with something glad - a laugh, a gratitude, a small good moment.

### A day with the Guide, a day without the Guide

**The learner's day runs 9:00 am to 1:30 pm.**

**A Launch day (with the Guide), 9:00 - 1:30:**

| Time | Block | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| 9:00 - 10:00 | Walk-in & Project Hour | Arrive at your own pace; self-directed work - reading, core academics, goal-setting. A soft, working start. The Guide is present and available, not directing. |
| 10:00 - 10:25 | Opening Circle | The settle (body-first) + "how are you, really?" The cohort gathers; presence before content. |
| 10:25 - 10:40 | The Quest Frame | The Guide opens the session's quest as a question, not a lecture. |
| 10:40 - 11:45 | The Work of the Day | The core activity - this is the slot that changes per session. Learner-led; the Guide holds questions, not answers. |
| 11:45 - 12:15 | The Table | A shared meal - the joy and belonging practice. |
| 12:15 - 1:10 | Apply & Collaborate | Carry the quest into hands-on and collaborative work; project application. |
| 1:10 - 1:30 | The Bridge + Glad Close | Name the next step to carry into project days (a self-chosen proximal goal); a light close. |

*Why open with a working hour:* it gives latecomers grace, eases learners in through self-direction before a social circle (a gift for the anxious or introverted), and signals that even a guided day is the learner's own. The settle-and-circle still opens the *together* work; the glad close still bookends it.

**A project day (without the Guide), same 9:00 - 1:30 window, self-directed:**

| Flow | What happens |
|---|---|
| Self-set intention | The learner names today's focus - the next step they chose at the Launch's Bridge. |
| Deep-work blocks | Reading, core academics / HS requirements, the personal project, and the season's writing. 90-minute cycles. |
| Peer collaboration | Optional - problems and projects are often built with others. |
| The Log | Capture the day in the Captain's Log (theirs alone). |

The Guide is off the floor on these days - available if asked, never directing. That absence is the lesson: a young person learns they can hold their own hours. On cadence, a project day *is* the fallow / hearth day - rest, play, friendship - protected and never converted back into work.

### The Captain's Log (the constant thread)

One learner-owned artifact, carried and deepened across all three seasons. The learner names it themselves. It survives rotation: the order of seasons changes what they write *about*; it never changes that they are writing. **It belongs to the learner. A guide never reads it for assessment** - the moment it becomes surveillance, the trust architecture collapses.

### The Next Great Adventure (the graduation rite, off the cycle)

The send-off is not tied to a season. It is the learner's **personal graduation rite**, overlaid on whatever season they leave from. Every graduating captain gets their launch - one Next Great Adventure, at the true end of *their* line. (A learner who meets the Resolution season early rehearses the "what's next" question years before they answer it for real. That is practice, not redundancy.)

### Each season keeps its own lens-true harvest (Session 7)

| Season | Session 7 harvest |
|---|---|
| The Set-Up | **The Unveiling** - self-portrait / "This Is Me" exhibition |
| The Problem | **The Call to Action** - public advocacy talk: a problem worth solving + their service response |
| The Resolution | **The Council** - public deliberation of a real moral dilemma / leadership demonstration |
| *Any season, a learner's final year* | **Your Next Great Adventure** - personal graduation launch |

## The "Live & Love Life" thread (joy woven in, not bolted on)

Joy is part of the formation, not the reward for it. It runs as the **register** of the work, plus **protected unstructured space**, and the Guide **models** a life worth loving. It is invitational and unmeasured: joy assigned is joy destroyed.

**Each season has its own flavor of joy, tied to its lens:**

| Season | Its joy |
|---|---|
| The Set-Up | **Roots & belonging** - the foods, music, and stories of your lineage; wonder at your own story; play with who you are |
| The Problem | **Curiosity & company** - the delight of the "aha"; solving *with* friends; expeditions out into the world; the warmth of service done together |
| The Resolution | **Expression & camaraderie** - theatre and the stage as play; the fellowship of leading together; awe at big questions; the adult joy of choosing life *because* you have looked at hard things |

**Recurring practices (the register, present in every season):**

- **Expeditions** - regular real-world and outdoor adventures. These are teenagers; get them moving, in nature, in the world. Adventure for its own sake, not only to study something.
- **The Table** - shared meals as ritual. A regular communal meal, and a feast at every harvest.
- **The Arts** - music, movement, making, theatre present throughout, not quarantined in one season.
- **Belonging** - cohort friendship rituals; celebrating each other, not only individual work.
- **The Fallow Day** - protected, celebrated, never filled.
- **The Glad Close** - Launches can end with something light.

**Guard rails:** invited, never assigned; unmeasured, never graded; the fallow stays fallow; the Guide lives it rather than instructs it. The Guide's own joy is part of the curriculum, which is why Guide sustainability is in the plan.

---

## The six refusals (the floor under everything)

From the 2026-06-14 quest-cycle research brief. All of Launch Pad sits inside these:

1. No grades or quantified completion rates visible to anyone but the learner
2. No ranking, leaderboards, or peer-visible progress
3. No banking-model knowledge deposit - the learner co-creates meaning; the Guide does not author it for them
4. No external deadline imposition - the learner sets pace; "I didn't get to it" is honored
5. No productivity-as-worth framing - mastery language; an abandoned-with-learning quest is honored equally with a completed one
6. No audit-culture surveillance - the data belongs to the learner, not the school

Plus the **right to refuse the structure itself**: a learner who declines a quest is fully honored, not coded as opt-out failure.

## Locked decisions

1. **Three lenses, not three grades.** Each season is a complete hero's journey containing identity + problem + resolution at its own altitude; rotation works because no season is a prerequisite.
2. **One constant thread.** The learner-owned Captain's Log, deepened across all three seasons, surviving rotation. Doubles as Jenna's one tangible object.
3. **Joy & rest are structural.** Regular fallow/hearth day; body-first settle + "how are you, really?" to open; harvests are real celebrations.
4. **Resume reframed.** The Set-Up produces a "story-of-self portfolio"; the resume is one true page inside it, framed as a sovereignty tool, not the season's reduction.
5. **Year-3 safety architecture is binding.** Allegory-first; opt-out without explanation; no required self-disclosure; dilemmas resolve into *reasoning*, never "the right answer"; Guide trained to pivot.
6. **Guide sustainability in the plan.** Supervision, peer support, and rest for the Guide written into what Jenna sees. Guide = life-coach with permission to not-know.
7. **The six refusals + right-to-refuse-structure govern all of it.**
8. **One Next Great Adventure, and it is a graduation rite** - lifted off the cycle, overlaid on each learner's final year.
9. **Each season keeps a lens-true harvest** - Unveiling / Call to Action / Council. No season borrows another's ending.
10. **"Live & Love Life" is a woven thread, not a wrapper** - per-season joy-flavor + recurring practices + Guide models it, all invitational and unmeasured.
11. **Set-Up Sessions 5 & 6 split as expansion to distillation** - S5 "The Full Draft" (claim the whole life, no shaping); S6 "Chapter One" (author's craft; keep the load-bearing, cut-to-focus-not-to-hide, the rest kept in the Captain's Log, second distillation into the story-of-self / resume page). "Chapter one" framed as *hope* - proof the book is not finished.
12. **Family-story safety net (binding for Set-Up S2 & S5)** - family = who made you (chosen/found included); opt-out without explanation; heavy material previewed with a real exit; potluck is belonging not performance.
13. **The Next Great Adventure - separate the rite from the question.** The *rite* (graduation send-off) stays bound to each learner's final year, whatever season that is - one send-off at the true end of their line. The *question* ("what is your next great adventure?") opens as a standing invitation from any season, any time, especially for graduating learners. Season Three rehearses the question for everyone without it being anyone's premature send-off.

## SEASON ONE: ORIGIN (the set-up) - deep build

*Lens: Who am I? Joy-flavor: roots & belonging.*
*"The soul never thinks without a picture." - Aristotle*

**What your learner walks away with** *(share with parents at the start of the year):*
- A **story-of-self portfolio**, with a one-page resume inside it
- Real **confidence in where they came from and who they are now**
- **100 dreams named** - and the courage to dream bigger
- Practical footing: **time management, communication, routine**, and progress on HS requirements
- The first chapters of their **Captain's Log**, carried through all three years

**The season's secret engine (tell Jenna this):** Covey - *"All things are created twice; first mentally, then physically. The key to creativity is to begin with the end in mind."* The Set-Up is the **first creation** - dreaming it. The Problem and Resolution seasons are the **second** - building it.

The 7 sessions (4 short at 3-4 weeks, 3 long at about 6 weeks; Session 7 is the harvest):

### Session 1 - "The Map of Me" *(short)*
- **Focus:** The 100 Dreams meeting. Dream big dreams with courage. First sort the 100 by time horizon - more than 5 years out, within 5 years, and within 1 year - then map them across all 12 life categories, so it is not only career but friendship, health, adventure, family, faith, learning, contribution.
- **Anchor:** Covey - created twice, begin with the end in mind. This dream-set is the mental creation the whole cycle will physically build toward. The far-horizon dreams are the "end" to begin with; the within-1-year dreams become the season's first proximal goals.
- **Launches:** Guide runs the 100 Dreams practice (write 100, no filtering); sort them by horizon (more than 5 years / within 5 years / within 1 year); then map across the 12 life categories; a Socratic courage pass - "which of these scares you a little? Courage points there."
- **Project days:** begin the Captain's Log; start core academics / HS requirements rhythm.
- **Joy:** the thrill of permission to dream without a ceiling; wonder at possibility.

### Session 2 - "The Lineage" *(short)*
- **Focus:** The family tree as **mythos** - the stories of your lineage gathered the way deep culture is gathered. Family interviews. The recipes and foods of where you come from.
- **Listening model:** the *Midnight Gospel* "Mortality and the Mind" episode (Duncan Trussell interviewing his mother weeks before her death) - a masterclass in an ordinary life carrying enormous meaning. Preview it; watch the room.
- **Harvest-within:** a **family-story potluck** - everyone brings a dish and a story.
- **Launches:** how to gather a story; how to interview someone you love; how to listen for the story under the facts.
- **Project days:** conduct interviews; gather recipes; write up family stories into the Log.
- **Joy:** the potluck feast; the foods and music of lineage; belonging.
- **Safety (binding):** family = who made you (biological, chosen, found - mentors, grandparents, a coach, a sibling all count). Opt-out on any interview with no explanation owed. The mortality episode previewed, with somewhere to step out *to*. The potluck is belonging, not performance - a store-bought dish is fully welcome.

### Session 3 - "The Power of Choice" *(short)*
- **Focus:** Choice as the engine of a life. The relationship between responsibility, freedom, and choice as one knot, not three topics.
- **Practice:** start each day with **choice games** - small, playful, real decisions and their consequences, so choosing becomes muscle.
- **Launches:** the choice games; Socratic work on "where did a choice change everything?"
- **Project days:** personal projects; HS requirements; Log entries on choices made.
- **Joy:** play - the games are genuinely fun; agency feels good in the body.
- **Sets up:** Sessions 5-6, where shaping your own story *is* the power of choice applied to narrative.

### Session 4 - "Running My Own Ship" *(short)*
- **Focus:** Time management; communication; finishing HS requirements. A **midpoint reflection** on Sessions 1-3 and how the learner is evolving. Routine.
- **The frame to teach:** routine is not the opposite of freedom - it is what makes freedom possible. Responsibility, freedom, and choice, lived as a rhythm that fits *you*.
- **Launches:** building a routine that holds; communication practice; the look-back ("what have these first weeks shown you about yourself?").
- **Project days:** catch up / get ahead on HS requirements; refine the personal-project plan.
- **Joy:** the quiet satisfaction of a rhythm that fits; a clear desk; momentum.

### Session 5 - "The Full Draft" *(long) - EXPANSION*
- **Focus:** Claim your whole life. Write all of it - messy, complete, unedited. No shaping yet. Nothing is too small. This is the autobiography in full.
- **Why first:** they need the relief of getting it all down before they can bear to start choosing what to keep.
- **Scaffolding prompts (the Guide opens the draft with these):**
  - *Turning points:* "Name a moment after which you were not the same. What changed?"
  - *Sensory doorways:* a place, a smell, a song, a meal, a room - memory lives in the body before it lives in words.
  - *The people:* "Who made you? Not just family - anyone who left a mark."
  - *The hard and the best:* the worst day, the best day, and what each taught.
  - *A slice of life:* "Write one ordinary day at age ___, start to finish." (This is the "slices of life.")
- **Listening models (how an ordinary life becomes a story):** StoryCorps (three-minute real lives), memoir excerpts, the *Midnight Gospel* episode. Coming-of-age songs as **mirrors, not molds** - "which line is about you?" then back to their own words.
- **Joy:** the relief and catharsis of claiming a whole life; being witnessed.
- **Safety:** the same family-story net applies - some lives carry loss; opt-out on any memory.

### Session 6 - "Chapter One" *(long) - DISTILLATION*
- **Focus:** Become the author. A chapter is not a smaller version of your life - it is a shaped thing with a throughline. Learn to find it.
- **The reframe that makes it land for a teenager:** to a 16-year-old, this *is* their whole life - so do not argue with that, flip it. **Shrinking it to "chapter one" is an act of hope: it declares there are chapters coming.** It does not make their life small; it makes their future big.
- **The truth-discipline (teach explicitly):** we never cut to hide, we cut to **focus**. What you leave out of chapter one is not denied or erased - it is *kept*, in the full draft, in the Captain's Log. It is still true. It just is not load-bearing for *this* chapter's shape. Cutting is choosing what matters most right now - the power of choice from Session 3, turned into craft.
- **The shrink mechanic (the Guide holds this):**
  1. From the full draft, find the **throughline** - "what is this chapter *about*?" (One sentence.)
  2. Choose the **3-5 load-bearing moments** that set up who you are becoming. *Keep, because* - not *cut, because*.
  3. Write **Chapter One** - tight, shaped, true.
  4. Everything cut goes into the **Captain's Log** as the full draft - kept, honored, not lost.
  5. Distill the *same life* into a **second shape: the one-page story-of-self / resume.** Same material, two forms - a *story* (meaning) and a *page* (practical). Two answers to "who are you," for two different rooms. This is also what makes the resume different from the autobiography: not a smaller story, a different *kind* of distillation.
- **Joy:** the craft delight of shaping; the hope of "chapters to come."

### Session 7 - "This Is Me" / The Unveiling *(long) - HARVEST*
- **Focus:** A self-portrait / art piece. A "This Is Me" piece. Present who-you-are, the story-of-self portfolio, the resume page, Chapter One.
- **The bookend:** dreamed in S1, drafted in S5, shaped in S6, *declared* in S7. If the closing anthem is "This Is Me," the year rhymes from end to end.
- **Form:** a real celebration with a real audience - a feast, not a presentation.
- **Joy:** being seen by your people; the unveiling feast.

### Origin at the day level

Every session runs the same two-day skeleton - a Launch day (9:00 to 1:30, with the Guide) and self-directed project days. Only the Launch's *Work of the Day* and the project focus change. Here is that change across Origin's seven sessions:

| Session | Launch "Work of the Day" (across the session's weeks) | Project-day focus |
|---|---|---|
| S1 The Map of Me | 100 Dreams unfiltered; sort by horizon (5+ years / within 5 years / within 1 year); then map across the 12 life categories; "which dreams scare you?" courage pass; Covey's created-twice frame | Draft the 100 dreams; open the Captain's Log; pick 3 within-1-year dreams to orient the season; reading, core / HS |
| S2 The Lineage | How to gather a story and interview someone you love; the Midnight Gospel listen (previewed, exit open); map the family mythos; the family-story potluck | Conduct family interviews; gather recipes; write family stories into the Log; reading, core / HS |
| S3 The Power of Choice | Daily choice games; the responsibility-freedom-choice knot; "where did a choice change everything?" | Choice journaling in the Log; personal project; goal-setting practice; core / HS |
| S4 Running My Own Ship | Build a routine that fits you (routine = freedom); communication practice; midpoint reflection on S1-3 | Catch up or get ahead on HS requirements; design your own routine; refine the personal-project plan |
| S5 The Full Draft | The scaffolding prompts (turning points, sensory doorways, the people, hard and best, a slice of life); listening models (StoryCorps, memoir, coming-of-age songs as mirrors); write-it-all | Write the full autobiography draft (lots of writing time); read the models; reading, core / HS |
| S6 Chapter One | What a chapter is (throughline); the "chapter one = hope" reframe; the cut-to-focus truth-discipline; the shrink mechanic | Do the shrink; write Chapter One; build the story-of-self portfolio and the one-page resume |
| S7 This Is Me / The Unveiling | Make the self-portrait / "This Is Me" art piece; prepare the exhibition; the bookend anthem; the Unveiling feast | Build the art piece; finalize the portfolio; rehearse the unveiling |

## SEASON TWO: THE FORGE (the problem) - deep build

*Lens: what is worth solving? Joy-flavor: curiosity & company.*
*"Things are only impossible until they're not." - Jean-Luc Picard*

**What your learner walks away with** *(share with parents at the start of the year):*
- A **real problem they chose** because it matters to them
- The skill to **name a problem precisely and trace it to its root**
- A **service or philanthropy project** they acted on, with real people
- Experience **collaborating and failing forward**
- A public **Call to Action talk** that can move a room

This is where Origin's dreaming becomes building - the **second creation** Covey named.

**The season's signature daily ritual:** the **Impossible Question.** Year One opened each morning with choice games; Year Two opens with a question that has no clean answer, debated playfully ("Could we end loneliness?" "Can one person change a system?"). The point is never to answer it - it is to grow comfortable standing inside something too big to close. Run it all season; intensify it in Session 5.

The 7 sessions (4 short, 3 long; Session 7 is the harvest):

### Session 1 - "Naming the Dragon" *(short)*
- **Focus:** the distinction nobody hands teenagers - a **problem** (you can act on it), a **fact of life** (accept it), a **preference** (you just want it), a **mystery** (sit with it). You fight a dragon differently depending which it is.
- **Games / teaching:**
  - *"Problem or Not?"* - a sorting game. Cards with real situations sorted into the four buckets. The arguments over the gray cards are the lesson.
  - *"Reframe It" to "How Might We"* - crank a complaint ("history is pointless") into a design-thinking question ("how might we make this matter?"). A complaint is a closed door; a How-Might-We is an open one.
  - *The Iceberg* - surface problem vs. the thing underneath (sets up the Detective).
- **Teaching image:** a vague dragon ("everything sucks") cannot be fought; a named one can. Naming precisely is half the victory.
- **Joy:** the relief of turning a fog of dread into a thing with a name.

### Session 2 - "The Detective" *(short)*
- **Focus:** investigation as root-cause analysis - observe, gather evidence, separate symptom from cause, keep asking *why* (the 5 Whys) until you hit bedrock. The obvious culprit is almost never the root cause.
- **The session is itself an act of choice - the cohort picks one of three formats:**

| Option | What they do | What it teaches best |
|---|---|---|
| (a) The Ready Mystery | Guide brings a finished whodunit; learners are detectives | The investigation skill, fast - pure root-cause practice |
| (b) Build-Your-Own | Learners design a mystery for another group to solve | The deepest - to write a mystery you must think like the system that made it |
| (c) The Speakeasy | An immersive, roam-the-room whodunit; clues hidden in the space | Experiential and kinesthetic; a first taste of theatre (foreshadows Year 3) |

- **A ready mystery for Option A - "A Death at Greywater Manor":**
  - *Setup:* Eleanor Vance, celebrated food entrepreneur and town philanthropist, found dead after a dinner party mid-storm, manor cut off. Six guests, each a motive and a secret.
  - *Suspects:* the Business Partner (about to be bought out), the Estranged Daughter (cut from the will), the Head Chef (publicly humiliated that night), the Town Councillor (about to be exposed), the Old Friend (secretly bankrupt, owed money), the Journalist (here to break a story about Eleanor's company).
  - *The play:* detectives interview suspects (peers or Guide), examine clues, build theories. Every suspect looks guilty in turn - that is the trap.
  - *The twist (the teaching):* run the 5 Whys. Why did she die? A reaction to her food. Why? The kitchen's allergen labeling failed. Why? Staff overworked and undertrained. Why? Eleanor had gutted the kitchen budget to fund the buyout. Why? Her company was bleeding money after being cited for contaminating the town's water. **No one murdered Eleanor. A system did.** The obvious villain is a symptom; the root cause is the rot underneath.
  - *The transfer:* the Guide then asks each learner to name a real problem and run the same 5 Whys - who is the obvious suspect, what is the system underneath?
- **Safety:** keep it Clue-style, cartoonish, bloodless - no graphic violence. Offer a lighter swap ("who sabotaged the harvest festival," "who killed the river") for any learner for whom a murder lands wrong. Same mechanics, same systemic twist.
- **Joy:** company and play; teens love this.

### Session 3 - "My Arena" *(short)*
- **Focus:** right-sizing. Covey's **Circle of Concern / Influence / Control.** Energy spent only in the Concern ring burns out into cynicism; the shift is to find the arena where action actually moves something.
- **What it looks like:**
  - *The Three Circles map* - list everything you worry about; sort into Control / Influence / Concern-only; watch where the energy can actually act.
  - *Agency inventory* - what you concretely have right now: skills, time, relationships, resources.
  - *The starter quest* - each learner picks one small real problem inside their arena and acts on it during project days. A win they can feel (proximal goal, self-efficacy spiral).
- **Ties to Year One's Power of Choice:** the move from "problems happen to me" to "I can act on this slice."
- **Joy:** the quiet power of "this one is mine, and I can move it."

### Session 4 - "The Wider Field" *(short)*
- **Focus:** lift their eyes from their own arena to the world's. They **choose the problem that matters to them** - never assigned. The finding question is not "what is important?" but *"what breaks your heart?"* (Buechner: where your deep gladness meets the world's deep hunger.)
- **What it looks like:**
  - *An expedition* - go out to where a real problem lives (a shelter, a riverbank, a food bank, a council meeting). See it firsthand. The season's joy-flavor made real.
  - *The Problem Gallery* - a walk past real-world problems (the UN goals, local issues, environmental, social). Which one will not let them go?
  - *Stakeholder intro* - who actually has this problem? (Sets up Session 6's empathy work.)
  - Choose the problem they carry through Sessions 5-7.
- **Safety:** world problems can drown a teenager in despair (eco-anxiety, doom). Always pair the wider field with agency - you do not have to solve all of it; you pick your slice. Hope, not helplessness. The expedition should leave them lit up, not crushed.

### Session 5 - "The Long Game" *(long)*
- **Focus:** can big problems be solved? Picard's line is the title-card. Big problems are **systems, not single acts** - no hero solves them alone. You break the impossible into the next doable step (proximal goals), find the leverage point, and collaborate.
- **The Impossible Question intensifies here** - this is the season ritual at full strength.
- **Three doors into the impossible (modalities - the Guide chooses how far to open each):**
  - *Clowning / play* - the clown faces what is too big by making it approachable, and fails constantly while turning failure into delight. This is **failing-forward** embodied: improv, "yes-and," permission to be gloriously wrong. The safest of the three and the best regulator - lead with it.
  - *The inner child* - children ask "why not?" before anyone teaches them a thing is impossible. Reconnects them to fearless curiosity.
  - *The shadow* - the uncomfortable truth inside a problem that nobody wants to name. Most big problems stay unsolved because of what everyone is avoiding.
- **Safety (binding):** aim shadow and inner-child work at **the shadow of the problem, not forced excavation of the self.** Ask "what is the uncomfortable truth inside this issue?" - never "what is your childhood wound?" Opt-out always available. Shadow comes last, lightest touch, fully invitational.
- **Content under the play:** systems thinking, leverage points, collaboration, iteration, failing forward.
- **Joy:** clowning; the company of collaborators; the awe of big questions.

### Session 6 - "Hands to Work" *(long) - finish line*
- **Focus:** real action on the chosen problem. **Present the choice** - do not assign service; offer a menu and let the learner choose how they serve. Assigned service teaches compliance; chosen service teaches contribution.

| Mode | What it is |
|---|---|
| Direct service | Hands-on, face-to-face with people affected |
| Advocacy | Raising the alarm, changing minds |
| Philanthropy | Giving - time and skill, not only money |
| Building | Making a thing that helps |
| Teaching | Passing on what you know to others facing the problem |

- **Core skill:** empathy - design *with* the people who have the problem, never *for* them. Talk to real, affected humans.
- **Safety:** do not place teenagers in emotionally over-their-head situations with vulnerable populations without support. Service should stretch, not wound.
- **Joy:** the warmth of service done together.

### Session 7 - "The Call to Action" *(long) - harvest*
- **Focus:** the public advocacy talk. Each learner makes the case: here is a problem worth solving, here is why it broke my heart, here is what I did and learned, here is what I am asking of you. This is **advocacy, not a sales pitch** (distinct from Year Three's venture pitch) - the art is making a room care.
- **Structure to teach (simply):** Problem to Stakes to Story to The Ask. Vulnerability is the persuasive force, not polish.
- **Form:** a feast, not a presentation. Real audience; the cohort cheers each other.
- **Joy:** being heard; the company of a cohort that has your back.

## SEASON THREE: THE HELM (the resolution) - deep build

*Lens: what do I do when there is no easy answer? Joy-flavor: expression & camaraderie.*
*"The first duty of every officer is to the truth." / The Kobayashi Maru: the test was never how to win - it was how to face a no-win scenario without losing yourself.*

**What your learner walks away with** *(share with parents at the start of the year):*
- The capacity to **make a hard call from principle** - and live with it
- Practice **reasoning through real moral dilemmas** (not memorizing answers)
- A **professional mentor** in their field of interest
- **Leadership experience** - leading peers, and being led well
- A public **Council**: deliberating a real dilemma in front of an audience

**Binding safety floor (applies to the whole season):** everything runs through **allegory**; opt-out on anything with no explanation owed; no learner is ever required to disclose their own experience to participate; dilemmas resolve into **reasoning, never "the right answer"**; the Guide is trained to watch the room and pivot. Held, this season transforms; unheld, it wounds. Specific danger points are flagged per session.

The 7 sessions (4 short, 3 long; Session 7 is the harvest):

### Session 1 - "The Mantle" *(short) - imposter games*
- **Focus:** Who are you to solve problems? Why you? Imposter feelings spike in adolescence *for a reason* - they arrive when identity forms and stakes rise. Not a flaw; the feeling of reaching past your current self. Name that first.
- **The imposter games:**
  - *The Fraud Confession* - everyone shares a time they felt like a fraud; Guide goes first; invite a mentor. Seeing accomplished adults feel it dissolves its power. (Opt-out - shame lives close for some.)
  - *"Why not you?"* - flip every "who am I to do this" into "who else, if not you?"
  - *The Evidence File* - counter the imposter's lie with real evidence of competence (pulls from the Year-One portfolio).
  - *The Mantle ceremony* - put on a cloak/role and act with authority you do not yet feel. The lesson lands in the body: act before you feel ready, and worthiness follows.
- **The teaching:** worthiness is chosen, not earned. Leadership never required certainty; it required acting rightly inside uncertainty.
- **Joy:** the play of stepping into a role bigger than you feel.

### Session 2 - "No Easy Answers" *(short) - TNG + the great cases of history*
- **Focus:** moral dilemmas through TNG allegory, each tied to a real human case. The allegory is the safety feature - a room can debate personhood through Data's trial without anyone confessing a thing.

| TNG episode | The dilemma | Real case to tie it to |
|---|---|---|
| The Measure of a Man | Is Data property or a person? | Dred Scott; abolition; modern AI/personhood |
| The Drumhead | Security vs. liberty; the witch-hunt | McCarthyism; Salem; post-9/11 surveillance |
| I, Borg | Do you use an enemy as a weapon? | The atomic bomb decision; total war |
| Symbiosis | Aid vs. non-interference; dependency | Colonialism; humanitarian intervention |
| Justice | One punishment for any crime; mercy vs. law | Mandatory minimums; proportionality |
| The First Duty | Truth vs. loyalty; the cover-up | Whistleblowing; institutional loyalty |

- **The win is reasoning, not the answer.** Introduce frameworks lightly - consequences, duties, character, care - and Kohlberg's stages. Holding the tension and reasoning from principle is arrival; a tidy verdict usually skipped the lesson.
- **Safety (flag):** preview-and-alternate the heaviest - *Ethics* (assisted death), *The Outcast* (identity/conformity), *Chain of Command* (coercion). Keep alternates ready; opt-out stands.
- **Joy:** the thrill of a real argument among friends who stay friends.

### Session 3 - "The Ripple" *(short) - what-if games*
- **Focus:** consequence-thinking; thinking several moves ahead; second- and third-order effects. Good captains think in ripples, not points.
- **The what-if games:**
  - *The Ripple Board* - pose a "what if" and map outcomes in waves: 1st-order (immediate), 2nd-order (then what?), 3rd-order (then what?). Chasing the cascade is the game.
  - *The Monkey's Paw* - every wish granted with an unforeseen cost; the shadow side of "solutions."
  - *Counterfactual history* - "what if X never happened?" Watch systems ripple.
  - *The Pre-Mortem (ties Year 2 to Year 3)* - imagine last season's solution succeeded wildly and then caused a new problem. What is the shadow of your fix?
- **Joy:** the delight of chasing a cascade to absurd and revealing places.

### Session 4 - "The Stage" *(short) - theatre*
- **Focus:** theatre as embodiment and perspective-taking - stepping into another life, especially a hard one. The stage's distance is a safety feature, like allegory: you can *be* the person who makes the other choice.
- **The keystone text - "Our Town" (Wilder):** its question - *"Do any human beings ever realize life while they live it?"* - is the "live and love life" thread spoken from the stage. Emily returns from death to relive one ordinary day. It rhymes with the Year-One mortality work.
- **Also:**
  - *Monologue work* - each learner finds or writes a monologue from someone facing a hard call (TNG, history, literature). Speaking another's truth from the inside.
  - *Archetypes* - Hero, Mentor, Shadow, Trickster, Ruler, Caregiver as roles to step into, not become. "I encountered the Hero," never "I am the Hero." A doorway, not an identity.
  - *Forum Theatre (Boal)* - act out a dilemma, then let the audience stop the scene and try a different choice. Deliberation made physical; feeds Session 7's Council.
- **Safety (flag):** learners choose their roles and can opt out of any; close with a **de-roling ritual** (stepping cleanly out of a heavy character). *Our Town*'s death scene can land on real grief; hold it.
- **Joy:** expression; the play of becoming someone else for an hour.

### Session 5 - "The Arena of Voices" *(long) - professional mentors*
- **Focus:** finding and working with real professional mentors in the field the learner has circled all cycle. Being mentored is a threshold - being seen by someone you admire.
- **What it looks like:**
  - *Mentor matching* - connect each learner with a working professional in their area of interest.
  - *The art of being mentored (a real, teachable skill)* - how to reach out, ask a question worth a busy person's time, follow up. (The "read their published work before first contact" discipline belongs here.)
  - *The informational interview* - and note: interviewing is now a through-skill across all three years (family, stakeholders, mentors).
  - *The pitch* - present their thinking to real professionals; receive real feedback. A mentor is a different kind of guide: not the life-coach, but someone living the life the learner is curious about.
- **Joy:** the company of someone further down a road you want to walk.

### Session 6 - "The Bridge" *(long) - group leadership - finish line*
- **Focus:** leading peers; the captain's chair. Leadership is stewardship, not command - the measure of a leader is what continues when they step back. Lead toward your own unnecessity.
- **What it looks like:**
  - *Rotating command* - everyone leads a real group task; everyone follows. Learn to lead by leading and by being led well.
  - *Find your style* - Picard, Kirk, Janeway, Sisko: four captains, four styles, all real. Which captain do you lead like?
  - *The hard parts* - feedback, the unpopular call, holding a group through conflict, delegating, good follower-ship.
  - *Expedition leadership* - a real group challenge outdoors where the chair rotates (joy: camaraderie + the outdoors).
- **Honor the quiet leaders:** leading softly from the back is a different leadership, not a weaker one. Do not let "leadership" collapse into "loudest."
- **Joy:** camaraderie; the fellowship of leading together.

### Session 7 - "The Council" *(long) - the hard call - harvest*
- **Focus:** a council is not a debate (a debate has a winner); it has discernment. Learners gather around a real dilemma and reason out loud, in front of their people - not performing certainty, but holding tension without collapsing it.
- **What it looks like:**
  - A real dilemma is put to the learners - a community question, a historical case, or a live ethical one. They deliberate publicly: reason from principle, name the cost, honor the dissent; a leader (mentored leadership demonstration) holds the room.
  - Feed in the Forum Theatre from Session 4 - act the dilemma, then council it.
  - The exhibition *is* the thinking, not a verdict. Witnesses see reasoning, not answers.
  - Leave the door open for the Kobayashi Maru move - the learner who refuses the binary and finds the third option. The captain's art, and the right note to end a formation on.
- **Safety:** the dilemma is allegorical/external, not personal; opt-out; no forced disclosure; a held container.
- **Form:** a feast, a real audience, the cohort holding each other.
- **Joy:** camaraderie; being witnessed reasoning honestly about something that matters.
